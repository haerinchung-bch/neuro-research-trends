## 1. Executive Summary

The following five findings represent the most significant contributions to the field this month and in recent weeks:

1. **Infants have rich visual categories in ventrotemporal cortex at 2 months of age** *(Nature Neuroscience, Feb 2026)*: O'Doherty, Cusack et al. (Trinity College Dublin) performed awake fMRI in over 100 two-month-old infants and found that categorical representations for 12 object classes are already established in high-level visual cortex. This overturns decades-old assumptions that high-level object categorization emerges only after months of visual learning, and demonstrates that representational geometry at 2 months closely mirrors adult patterns. DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41593-025-02187-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41593-025-02187-8)

2. **Neonatal fMRI predicts 18-month socioemotional outcomes** *(bioRxiv, May 2026)*: Zou & Bokde (Trinity College Dublin) used connectome-based predictive modeling on 397 neonates from the Developing Human Connectome Project to demonstrate that whole-brain resting-state connectivity at birth can prospectively predict externalizing behavior, surgency, and effortful control at 18 months. DOI: [https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.21.719787](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.21.719787)

3. **Explicitly nonlinear fMRI reveals hidden infant developmental trajectories** *(bioRxiv, April 2026)*: Kinsey, Iraji et al. (TReNDS / Georgia State University) made the first whole-brain nonlinear fMRI connectivity analysis in typically developing infants, uncovering networks invisible to conventional linear methods. DOI: [https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.07.716703](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.07.716703)

4. **Single-cell atlas of the developing Down syndrome cortex** *(Nature Medicine, January 2026)*: Lattke et al. profiled ~250,000 cells from 15 DS and 15 control fetal cortices and identified three chromosome 21 transcription factors as dosage-sensitive therapeutic targets. DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04211-1](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04211-1)

5. **Cascading sensitive periods for language-related brain plasticity** *(bioRxiv, March 2026)*: Ellwood-Lowe et al. (UC Berkeley / UPenn) mapped a posterior-to-anterior cascade of language-sensitive periods from 10 months to 15 years using Hurst exponent across two large cohorts. DOI: [https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.27.714739](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.27.714739)

**Paper 1**
**Title:** Tracing infant sleep neurophysiology longitudinally from 3 to 6 months: EEG insights into brain development

> 🔬 **EEG (high-density, sleep)** · **3–6 months** · **Sleep neurophysiology · Thalamocortical maturation · Plasticity**
> 📌 **Read if you study:** infant sleep EEG, cortical maturation markers, or thalamocortical development in the first year
> 💡 **In one sentence:** Slow-wave activity increased in occipital regions, sigma power shifted from central to frontal, and theta increased globally from 3 to 6 months — providing frequency-specific markers of cortical plasticity.

**Authors:** Matthieu Beaugrand, Valeria Jaramillo, Christophe Mühlematter, Sarah F. Schoch, Vivien Reicher, Andjela Markovic, Salome Kurth
**Journal:** *npj Biological Timing and Sleep* | **Date:** 2026 (Vol. 3, Art. 9) | **Preprint:** No
**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s44323-026-00071-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44323-026-00071-7)
**First Author Institution:** Donders Institute, Radboud University Medical Centre, Nijmegen, Netherlands
**Age Range:** 3–6 months (n=11 infants, 9 analyzable) | **Method:** EEG (high-density, NREM sleep) | **Statistical Approach:** Longitudinal EEG power analysis; scalp topographic mapping; mixed-effects modeling of SWA (0.75–4.25 Hz), theta (4.5–7.5 Hz), sigma (9.75–14.75 Hz)
**Key Findings:** Slow-wave activity (SWA) increased maximally in occipital regions from 3 to 6 months, while theta showed a global increase across all scalp regions. Sigma power, initially concentrated centrally, dispersed toward frontal regions — consistent with progressive thalamocortical maturation. These topographic developmental shifts provide frequency-specific markers of cortical plasticity in early infancy.
**Key Figure:** Topographic scalp maps showing SWA, theta, and sigma power at 3 vs. 6 months, with t-statistic overlays highlighting regions of significant longitudinal change.

**Paper 2**
**Title:** Effects of Early Infant Nutrition on Aperiodic Exponent From 2 to 12 Months Using EEG Analysis

> 🔬 **HD-EEG (resting-state)** · **2–12 months** · **Aperiodic slope · Nutrition · E/I balance**
> 📌 **Read if you study:** EEG biomarkers of early brain development, E/I balance maturation, or effects of early nutrition on the brain
> 💡 **In one sentence:** Breastfed infants had a higher aperiodic exponent than formula-fed infants at 3 months only — no feeding-group differences were found at any other age from 2 to 12 months.

**Authors:** Dylan Gilbreath, Adam Andrews, Darcy Hagood, Aline Andres, Linda J. Larson-Prior
**Journal:** *Psychophysiology* | **Date:** 2026-02-05 | **Preprint:** No
**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1111/psyp.70247](https://doi.org/10.1111/psyp.70247)
**First Author Institution:** Arkansas Children's Nutrition Center / University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences
**Age Range:** 2–12 months (7 timepoints) | **Method:** High-density EEG (resting-state) | **Statistical Approach:** FOOOF/specparam aperiodic decomposition; source-space reconstruction; linear mixed models by feeding group
**Key Findings:** Aperiodic exponent decreased with age across all infants (consistent with E/I balance maturation). Breastfed infants showed a significantly larger aperiodic exponent at 3 months only. One of the first studies to apply source-space reconstruction for spatial mapping of aperiodic activity in infants.
**Key Figure:** Aperiodic exponent trajectories 2–12 months per feeding group with confidence intervals; box plots highlighting the significant 3-month group difference.

**Paper 13**
**Title:** Cascading periods of language-related brain plasticity across early childhood

> 🔬 **fMRI (resting-state, Hurst exponent)** · **10 months–15 years** · **Language sensitive periods · Cortical plasticity · BCP + HCP-D**
> 📌 **Read if you study:** language network development, cortical sensitive periods, or longitudinal fMRI methods across childhood
> 💡 **In one sentence:** Language-related cortical areas mature in a posterior-to-anterior cascade from 10 months to 15 years, and stronger language skills are paradoxically associated with slower cortical Hurst increases in early childhood — consistent with protracted plasticity as a developmental advantage.

**Authors:** Monica E. Ellwood-Lowe, Monami Nishio, Alexander J. Dufford, Michael Arcaro, Theodore D. Satterthwaite, Allyson P. Mackey

**First Author Institution:** UC Berkeley / University of Pennsylvania
**Age Range:** 10 months–15 years (BCP: n=222; HCP-D: n=324) | **Method:** fMRI (Hurst exponent as plasticity index) | **Statistical Approach:** Growth curve modeling; cross-dataset validation; language skills regression
**Key Findings:** Posterior language regions mature before anterior; thalamic Hurst plateaus earliest. Better language skills associated with slower (protracted) cortical Hurst increases in early childhood, across two independent large datasets.
**Key Figure:** Cortical Hurst developmental trajectory maps with posterior-to-anterior gradient highlighted; scatter plots of Hurst vs. language scores per age group.

## 5a. Statistical Approaches Trending This Period

1. **FOOOF / specparam aperiodic decomposition** — The dominant shift this period. Multiple independent groups apply aperiodic decomposition to infant/toddler EEG, using the exponent as a proxy for E/I balance across autism risk, nutrition, maternal mental health, and normative development. Papers: 2, 4, 18, 19, 22.

2. **Connectome-based predictive modeling (CPM)** — Applied to neonatal fMRI for prospective prediction of 18-month outcomes. Stability-driven ROI-constrained CPM is establishing itself as the standard for early-life prognostic connectivity research. Papers: 6, 7.

3. **RSA and MVPA decoding** — O'Doherty et al. use RSA and cross-decoding to characterize categorical representations in infant ventrotemporal cortex. Paper: 5.

4. **Nonlinear and higher-order connectivity** — Kinsey et al. (nonlinear ICA) and Li et al. (tri-FNC tensor decomposition) demonstrate that standard linear methods miss major classes of developmentally structured fMRI connectivity. Papers: 8, 9.

5. **Hurst exponent / long-range temporal correlations** — Ellwood-Lowe et al. apply the Hurst exponent as an in vivo cortical plasticity proxy to map language sensitive period cascades. Paper: 13.

6. **Burst-resolved oscillatory analysis** — Vanneau et al. demonstrate that burst amplitude vs. temporal stability in alpha reveals mechanistic information about autism-related dysregulation missed by standard spectral power. Paper: 20.

7. **Functional connectome harmonics (FCH)** — FCH-derived entropy, power, and energy metrics as novel biomarkers of neonatal brain maturity (~30% of postmenstrual age variance). Paper: 10.

8. **dbWPLI for infant EEG connectivity** — Debiased weighted phase lag index recommended for infant EEG gamma connectivity to minimize volume conduction artifacts. Paper: 17.
