## 1. Executive Summary

Top highlights this period:

1. **Infants have rich visual categories in ventrotemporal cortex at 2 months** *(Nature Neuroscience)*: O'Doherty, Cusack et al. used awake fMRI in >100 two-month-old infants to show that categorical representations for 12 object classes are already established in high-level visual cortex, closely mirroring adult patterns. DOI: [10.1038/s41593-025-02187-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41593-025-02187-8)

2. **Income insufficiency impacts early EEG brain networks in infants** *(PNAS)*: Chung, Wilkinson, Nelson et al. (Boston Children's Hospital / Harvard) used network-based EEG analysis to show that income-related adversity alters resting-state oscillatory network topology from 4–12 months. DOI: [10.1073/pnas.2513598123](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2513598123)

3. **Caregiver-infant interactions shape functional connectivity at 1 month** *(bioRxiv, Lloyd-Fox / M. H. Johnson)*: Carnevali et al. (Birkbeck) combined fNIRS brain imaging with coded dyadic interactions at one month and found affectionate touch and caregiver engagement predicted stronger social brain network connectivity. DOI: [10.64898/2026.05.13.724776](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.05.13.724776)

4. **Explicitly nonlinear fMRI reveals hidden infant developmental trajectories** *(bioRxiv)*: Kinsey, Iraji et al. present the first whole-brain nonlinear ICA fMRI analysis in infants, uncovering sensorimotor, default mode, executive, and language networks invisible to standard linear methods. DOI: [10.64898/2026.04.07.716703](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.07.716703)

5. **Aperiodic EEG changes predict autism diagnosis and language at 18 months** *(Autism Research)*: Wilkinson, Chung, Nelson et al. (BCH/Harvard) show that greater increases in aperiodic offset and slope from 3–12 months predict both later autism diagnosis and worse language outcomes in high-risk infants. DOI: [10.1002/aur.70063](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.70063)

**Paper 16**
**Title:** Tracing infant sleep neurophysiology longitudinally from 3 to 6 months: EEG insights into brain development

> 🔬 **EEG (high-density, sleep)** · **3–6 months** · **Sleep neurophysiology · SWA · Thalamocortical maturation**
> 📌 **Read if you study:** infant sleep EEG, cortical maturation markers, or normative oscillatory development in the first year
> 💡 **In one sentence:** Slow-wave activity increased occipitally, sigma power shifted from central to frontal, and theta increased globally from 3 to 6 months — providing frequency-specific markers of thalamocortical development.

**Authors:** Matthieu Beaugrand, Valeria Jaramillo, Christophe Mühlematter, Sarah F. Schoch, Vivien Reicher, Andjela Markovic, Salome Kurth
**Journal:** *npj Biological Timing and Sleep* | **Date:** 2026-03-01 | **Preprint:** No

**First Author Institution:** University of Surrey, Surrey Sleep Research Centre
**Age Range:** 3–6 months | **Method:** EEG (high-density, NREM sleep) | **Statistical Approach:** EEG power spectral analysis; topographic mapping; longitudinal mixed-effects models
**Key Findings:** Developmental shifts in sleep EEG topography from 3 to 6 months provide normative benchmarks for sleep EEG maturation across frequency bands. SWA, theta, and sigma each showed distinct topographic trajectories consistent with progressive cortical and thalamocortical maturation.
**Key Figure:** Topographic maps of EEG power across sleep stages at 3 and 6 months.

**Title:** Effects of Early Infant Nutrition on Aperiodic Exponent From 2 to 12 Months Using EEG Analysis

## 6. Statistical Approaches Trending This Period

1. **FOOOF / specparam aperiodic decomposition** — Dominant shift across EEG and fMRI. Multiple groups apply aperiodic decomposition to infant/toddler EEG (papers 12, 24, 31, 34) and now also to BOLD fMRI signals (paper 25), linking the exponent to autism, nutrition, adversity, and normative development.

2. **Connectome-based predictive modeling (CPM)** — Applied to neonatal fMRI for prospective prediction of 18-month outcomes (papers 3, 7). Stability-driven ROI-constrained CPM is establishing itself as the standard for early-life prognostic connectivity research.

3. **Nonlinear and higher-order connectivity** — Kinsey et al. (nonlinear ICA; paper 4) and Li et al. (tri-FNC tensor decomposition; paper 28) demonstrate that standard linear correlation methods miss major classes of developmentally structured fMRI connectivity.

4. **Network-based graph-theoretic EEG analysis** — Chung et al. (paper 30) apply partial correlation networks and graph-theoretic metrics to longitudinal EEG, demonstrating network topology as a sensitive measure of adversity effects.

5. **Hurst exponent / long-range temporal correlations** — Ellwood-Lowe et al. (paper 9) apply Hurst exponent as an in vivo plasticity proxy to map language sensitive period cascades across two large longitudinal datasets.

6. **Spectral geometry on connectivity manifolds** — Paper 11 applies SPD correlation operators and Grassmann manifold features to resting-state fNIRS, achieving AUC=0.871 for bilingual/monolingual classification — a novel computational geometry approach.

7. **Burst-resolved oscillatory analysis** — Vanneau et al. (paper 5) demonstrate that decomposing EEG into burst amplitude vs. temporal stability reveals mechanistic information about autism-related alpha dysregulation lost in standard spectral power analysis.

8. **Functional connectome harmonics (FCH)** — Paper 10 introduces FCH-derived entropy, power, and energy metrics as novel neonatal brain maturity biomarkers explaining ~30% of postmenstrual age variance.
